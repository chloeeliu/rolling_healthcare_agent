Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) – Concise Summary

Definition:
AKI is an abrupt (hours to days) decline in kidney function, including both structural injury and functional impairment.

Core Diagnostic Criteria (KDIGO):
AKI is diagnosed if any of the following:
1. Increase in serum creatinine (sCr) ≥ 0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours
2. Increase in sCr ≥ 1.5× baseline within 7 days
3. Urine output < 0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥ 6 hours

Severity Staging:
- Stage 1: 1.5–1.9× baseline sCr or ≥0.3 mg/dL increase; UO <0.5 mL/kg/h (6–12h)
- Stage 2: 2.0–2.9× baseline sCr; UO <0.5 mL/kg/h (≥12h)
- Stage 3: ≥3× baseline sCr or sCr ≥4.0 mg/dL or RRT; UO <0.3 mL/kg/h (≥24h) or anuria ≥12h

Clinical Interpretation:
- Even small increases in sCr are clinically significant
- AKI reflects decreased GFR and impaired waste/fluid regulation
- Associated with high mortality and risk of chronic kidney disease

Key Features:
- Multifactorial etiology (often mixed):
  • Pre-renal (hypoperfusion)
  • Intrinsic (tubular, glomerular, interstitial, vascular injury)
  • Post-renal (urinary obstruction)
- sCr is an imperfect marker (delayed, influenced by non-renal factors)
- Urine output is a critical complementary signal

Pathophysiology:
- Common mechanisms: ischemia, nephrotoxicity, sepsis
- Cellular injury driven by ATP depletion, inflammation, oxidative stress
- Tubular cells (especially proximal) are most vulnerable

Special Context (Sepsis-related AKI):
- Occurs frequently in sepsis
- Driven by inflammation, microvascular dysfunction, cytokines
- Not purely ischemic → complex systemic process

Systemic Impact:
- AKI contributes to multi-organ dysfunction (lung, heart, liver, brain)
- Bidirectional organ crosstalk (e.g., cardiorenal syndrome)

Practical EHR Implementation:
- Monitor rolling sCr baseline (48h / 7-day window)
- Track urine output continuously
- Detect early-stage AKI (small sCr changes)
- Combine with context (e.g., sepsis, drugs, hypotension)
